Graph Theorem 0 - Proof Patterns

November 3, 2020

Graphs are often too large to interpret manually, so we use contrapositive proofs, contradiction proofs, and induction proofs to reason about arbitrary graphs. The Fibonacci numbers will also be covered as bonus.

Quick Guide

  1. Converse and Contrapositive
  2. Contradiction
  3. Ordinary Induction
  4. Strong Induction
  5. Recurrence

Converse and Contrapositive

Converse

The converse of “if $p$ the $q$” is “if $q$ then $p$”.
In logic notation: the converse of $p \Rightarrow q$ is $q \Rightarrow p$.
Generally, the converse is not logically equivalent to the original implication.

Contrapositive

The contrapositive of “if $p$ the $q$” is “if (not $q$) then (not $p$)”.
In logic notation: the contrapositive of $p \Rightarrow q$ is $\neg q \Rightarrow \neg p$.
Note that the contrapositive is logically equivalent to the original implication, which leads to a proof pattern that instead of the implication, prove its contrapositive.

Tips: the contrapositive doesn’t change the quantifiers like $\forall$ and $\exists$. For example, the contrapositive for

"for any integers $x,y$, if $xy$ is even then $x$ is even or $y$ is even"


is

"for any integers $x,y$, if $x$ is odd and $y$ is odd then $xy$ is odd".


Contradiction

A statement of the form “$P$ and (not $P$)” is called a contradiction. It is always false.
Here a new proof pattern is introduced that to prove “$P$” we can instead prove “if (not $P$) then $C$”, where $C$ stands for a statement that is a contradiction. This proof pattern can be justified by the logical equivalence that $p \equiv \neg p \Rightarrow F$.
Also, if we want to prove “if $P$ then $Q$” we can instead prove “if $P$ and (not $Q$) then $C$”, which is another proof pattern. This proof pattern is justified by the logical equivalence that $p \Rightarrow q \equiv p \wedge \neg q \Rightarrow F$. A proof example is given in note The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic
Note that any proof by contrapositive can be converted into a proof by contradiction.

Ordinary Induction

Induction is a useful proof pattern for statements in the form: “for all nature numbers $n$ we have $P(n)$”, where $P(n)$ is a predicate whose truth depends on $n$. In logical notation: $\forall n \in \mathbb{N}\ P(n)$.

Proof Pattern of Ordinary Induction

Let $P(n)$ be a predicate whose truth depends on $n$.
Proof pattern:
(Base Case) Check that $P(0)$ holds true.
(Induction Step) Let $k$ be an arbitrary natural number. Assume $\textbf{P(k)}.$ Using that derive $P(k+1)$.
Conclude $\forall n \in \mathbb{N} \ P(n)$.

The $P(k)$ inside the box in the induction step is called the Induction Hypothesis(IH). The IH must be stated inside the induction step because it refers to $k$.
In logical notation the induction step is $\forall k \in \mathbb{N}\ \ P(k) \Rightarrow P(k+1)$.

There is also a variant proof pattern for ordinary induction:
Let $n_0$ be a natural number and let $P(n)$ be a predicate that is well defined for all natural numbers $n\geq n_0$.
Proof pattern:
(Base Case) Check that $P(n_0)$ holds true.
(Induction Step) Let $k\geq n_0$ be an arbitrary natural number. Assume $\textbf{P(k)}.$ Using that derive $P(k+1)$.
Conclude $\forall n \geq n_0 \ P(n)$.
Induction is a powerful proof method in graph theorem. By doing induction on vertices or edges, problems can be solved.

Strong Induction

Let $n_0$ be a natural number and let $P(n)$ be a predicate that is well defined for all natural numbers $n\geq n_0$.
Proof pattern:
(Base Case) Drive/infer $P(n_0)$.
(Induction Step) Let $k \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $k \geq n_0$. Assume $P(n_0)$ and $\ldots$ and $P(k)$. Using that derive $P(k+1)$.
Conclude $\forall n \geq n_0 \ P(n)$.
The IH $P(n_0)$ and $\ldots$ and $P(k)$ is stronger than $P(k)$. But strong induction is mathematically equivalent to the ordinary induction.

Recurrence

The Fibonacci recurrence is given by

  • $F_0 = 0$
  • $F_1 = 1$
  • $F_n = F_{n-1} + F_{n-2} \ (n\geq 2)$

The Fibonacci recurrence can be solve with $F_n = \frac{(\psi ^n -\ \phi ^n)}{\sqrt{5}}$, where $\psi > \phi$ are the two roots of the equation $x^2 - x - 1 = 0$.
$\psi = \frac{(1+ \sqrt{5})}{2}$ is known as the Golden Ratio and $\phi = \frac{(1- \sqrt{5})}{2}$.

Some useful equations based on Fibonacci numbers
$F_{n-1} \cdot F_{n+1} - F_{n}^2 = (-1)^n$

$\sum_{i=0}^{n}i\cdot F_{2i} = (n+1)F_{2n+1}-F_{2n+2}$

$\sum_{i=0}^{n}F_i^2\cdot F_{i+1}=\frac{1}{2}F_{n+2}F_{n+1}F_{n}$
